Spock Adds Crash-Safe Multi-Master PostgreSQL Replication with Custom WAL RMGR

Spock implements multi-master PostgreSQL replication (version 15+) using a custom WAL RMGR, shared memory, and persistent snapshots, enabling lightweight, crash-safe progress tracking and schema consistency.

  • Spock provides logical multi-master PostgreSQL replication, requiring extension installation on each node, with tables needing identical schemas, columns, and constraints.
  • Building involves applying version-specific patches to PostgreSQL source, then configuring, compiling, and creating the extension; configuration includes setting wal_level, max_worker_processes, shared_preload_libraries, and enabling DDL replication.
  • The extension introduces a custom WAL resource manager (RMGR) that logs progress into WAL and shared memory, replacing catalog-based progress tracking, with persistence to $PGDATA/spock/resource.dat and compatibility views for existing functions.

Brainstem “Off Switch” May Unlock Chronic Pain Treatments

Research by J. Nicholas Betley and colleagues reveals a brainstem “off switch” involving Y1R neurons in the lateral parabrachial nucleus that suppresses long-term pain signals, offering new avenues for chronic pain treatment.

  • Nearly 50 million people in the U.S. suffer from chronic pain, which can last for decades and is often untreatable.
  • Research led by neuroscientist J. Nicholas Betley identifies a brainstem hub with a built-in “off switch” involving Y1 receptor (Y1R)-expressing neurons in the lateral parabrachial nucleus (lPBN) that modulate persistent pain signals.
  • Y1R neurons are activated during long-term pain and integrate signals related to hunger, fear, and thirst, with NPY signaling dampening pain transmission; these neurons are scattered across multiple cell types, suggesting a complex network.
  • Using calcium imaging in preclinical models, researchers observed that Y1R neurons exhibit tonic activity during enduring pain, akin to an engine idling, which may encode persistent pain states.
  • The discovery suggests potential for targeting these neurons as biomarkers for chronic pain and developing treatments that modulate brain circuits, including behavioral interventions like exercise and meditation.
